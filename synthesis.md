# Week 12 Synthesis

## Overview

Week 12 was four days of interrogating every engineering decision made in Weeks 10 and 11 until the decision could be defended from first principles. The deliverable was not a new system. It was the ability to explain what was actually built, where the claims are bounded, and what would have to be true for those claims to fail.

Ten gaps were closed across four topics: inference-time mechanics, agent and tool-use internals, training and post-training mechanics, and evaluation and statistics. Each gap closure produced a concrete edit to the Week 10/11 portfolio.

---

## Ten Gaps Closed

**Gap 1 — Cost driver identification.** The constrained prompt costs 73% more per task than baseline, but the mechanism was not understood. Output tokens are the dominant driver (approximately 88% of the cost delta), not input tokens. SimPO looks Pareto-optimal in part because the adapter produces shorter outputs, which reduces decode cost. The claim was defensible all along — but not for the reason the original ablation text implied.

**Gap 2 — Prefix caching scope.** Prefix caching was described as a general optimization available to the agent. It is model-dependent: Claude requires explicit `cache_control`, GPT-4o applies it automatically above 1,024 tokens, and Qwen3-Next does not support it at all due to its hybrid linear attention architecture. The system prompt at ~60 tokens is below the threshold for caching to fire on any provider regardless of configuration.

**Gap 3 — Model versus orchestrator planning.** The pipeline was documented as an agent using tools. It is not. `generation/service.py` sends no `tools` parameter. The model is a text rewriter (Pattern 2) and a governance gate (Pattern 3). Planning lives in Python. The word "agent" in the README was an overclaim that conflated three structurally distinct patterns.

**Gap 4 — Intent classification gap.** `route_inbound_message()` in `handoff.py` routes by token matching across six hardcoded lists. A reply of "Not interested, please remove me" contains none of the six opt-out tokens and routes to the scheduling branch. The prospect's intent is never read by the model. This is not a bug that can be fixed with better token lists. It requires a `classify_reply_intent` function-calling tool before the routing logic runs.

**Gap 5 — Behavioral learning versus vocabulary memorization.** The SimPO adapter was trained on 200 pairs built by `programmatic_template_v1`, with 82 unique input contexts. The SimPO loss goes down whether the model learned the behavioral rule (do not commit capacity you cannot deliver) or memorized the template vocabulary (these specific escalation words appear in chosen responses). A falling loss curve cannot distinguish the two. The paraphrase eval isolates the variable: same meaning, different words. If accuracy holds, the rule was learned. If it drops, the vocabulary was memorized.

**Gap 6 — In-distribution scope of the 82% claim.** The 82% capacity_honesty figure reflects performance on data generated by the same pipeline that produced the training set. The Pareto-optimality argument is valid within that template distribution. Whether the preference generalizes to novel phrasing is untested. The claim needed a scope statement, not a retraction.

**Gap 7 — ICC and score reliability.** The Automaton Auditor produces a 2.30-point range across 15 runs on the same repo. A single reported score is one sample from a distribution. ICC quantifies what fraction of total variance is between targets rather than within-target noise. With a 2.30-point spread on one target, ICC is near zero, meaning rank ordering is more noise than signal. SEM translates this into score units: under moderate reliability assumptions, a reported score of 3.00 covers a true range of 2.57 to 3.43.

**Gap 8 — Minimum runs for stable scoring.** Given an observed SD of 0.611, reaching a 95% CI within ±0.5 requires 9 runs. The 15 runs already collected are sufficient — the problem is they were never aggregated. The fix is reporting mean and CI, not collecting more data.

**Gap 9 — Paired bootstrap variance reduction.** Paired bootstrap CIs can be 85% narrower than unpaired on the same benchmark data. The mechanism is covariance cancellation: `Var(paired) = Var(A) + Var(B) - 2·Cov(A,B)`. When both models are evaluated on the same tasks, task-level difficulty creates high positive covariance. Pairing preserves it; unpaired bootstrap discards it. At n=59 tasks with task-level correlation of 0.97, the unpaired CI includes zero (3pp target undetectable) and the paired CI excludes zero (3pp target detectable). Same data, one structural difference.

**Gap 10 — When pairing can hurt.** The covariance cancellation benefit depends on positive covariance between the two conditions. This is always satisfied when two models are evaluated on the same task distribution, which is the relevant case for this benchmark. The general caveat (negative covariance makes pairing worse) is real but does not apply here.

## Most Surprising Finding

The most surprising finding was Gap 3: the system described as an agent using tools does not give the model tool schemas at any point in the main pipeline. The model has never been told functions exist. A proactive tool-use instruction in `method.md` was correct for the tau2-bench evaluation context (where the model received real schemas and was scored on tool call syntax) and entirely irrelevant to the production pipeline (where no schemas are passed). The same code, the same model, two structurally different roles — and the documentation collapsed them into one word.

This gap was the most surprising because it was not a matter of a number being bounded incorrectly or a claim requiring qualification. It was a category error in the architecture description. Closing it required replacing "agent using tools" with a precise account of three patterns that had been conflated.

## Canonical Reading List

**On inference-time mechanics:**
- Solovyeva and Castor, "Babbling" and Post-EOS Token Generation in LLM Outputs (2026) — the formal treatment of why models continue generating after completing the useful answer, and why output length constraints belong in the prompt rather than the tokenizer
- Anthropic prefix caching documentation — the definitive account of which models support caching, what token thresholds apply, and what silently breaks the cache

**On tool use and agent architecture:**
- Yao et al., "ReAct: Synergizing Reasoning and Acting in Language Models" (2022), arXiv:2210.03629 — the paper that formalized the plan-act-observe loop; reading it makes concrete the gap between Pattern 1 and an orchestrator-controlled pipeline
- Anthropic tool use documentation, docs.anthropic.com/en/docs/tool-use — the API-level spec for `tool_use` and `tool_result` content blocks

**On preference training:**
- Hong et al., "ORPO: Monolithic Preference Optimization without Reference Model" (2024), arXiv:2403.07691 — the ORPO loss formulation and why eval loss is not directly comparable to cross-entropy loss
- Azar et al., "A General Theoretical Paradigm to Understand Learning from Human Feedback" (2023), arXiv:2310.12036 — the theoretical grounding for SimPO and the margin-based objective

**On evaluation and statistics:**
- Efron and Tibshirani, "An Introduction to the Bootstrap" (1993) — the canonical derivation of paired bootstrap variance reduction
- Dror et al., "Deep Dominance — How to Properly Compare Deep Neural Models" (ACL 2019), arXiv:1811.01808 — why paired tests are required for benchmark comparisons in NLP and why binary detection rates violate the normality assumption
- Koo and Mae, "A Guideline of Selecting and Reporting Intraclass Correlation Coefficients for Reliability Research," Journal of Chiropractic Medicine (2016) — ICC threshold interpretations and how to select the right ICC variant for a multi-judge scoring system
- Zheng et al., "Judging LLM-as-a-Judge with MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena," NeurIPS 2023 — Section 4 on judge consistency across repeated runs; the empirical basis for treating LLM judge variance as a first-class measurement problem

## What the Week Changed About How I Work

The structural shift from Week 12 is not in any single gap — it is in the habit the week installed. Before this week, I would write a number like 82% or 73% and treat it as complete. The number existed. It came from code that ran. That felt like enough. What I did not have was the reflex to ask: what is this number actually measuring, what would have to be true for it to be wrong, and can I derive it from first principles if someone pushes back in a code review?

Every gap this week had the same shape underneath it: a claim that was directionally correct but could not be defended mechanically. The 73% cost delta was real — but I could not say whether it came from prefill or decode. The 82% accuracy was real — but I could not say whether it measured rule learning or vocabulary memorization. The architecture was working — but the word "agent" in the README was a category error. The paired bootstrap was implemented correctly — but I could not explain why pairing helped or what would have happened without it.

None of these were lies. They were gaps between what a system does and what its author understands about why it does that. Week 12 made those gaps visible and gave me the tools to close them.

The pairing structure is what made the gaps surface. It is harder to hide from a gap when you have to hand your question to another person and watch them try to answer it. If the question is vague, the explainer will be vague and the gap will not close. The morning call forcing a rewrite of the question is the mechanism — it turns a fuzzy unease into a diagnostic instrument. The gap formulation practice is transferable: open an artifact, read it as a hostile reviewer, ask where the language is papering over a mechanism you have not actually explained to yourself.

The grounding commits are the other structural element worth keeping. Each gap closure produced a concrete edit to existing work. Not a note to self. Not a planned future improvement. An actual change to an artifact that now reflects what I understand rather than what I assumed. That discipline — every learning produces an edit — is what prevents Week 12 from being a reading week that leaves the portfolio unchanged.

The five blog posts and threads are the public test. Writing for a reader who does not share your context forces a kind of precision that writing for yourself does not. The tweet thread especially: if you cannot compress the load-bearing mechanism into six tweets without losing the point, you do not understand it well enough yet. The Day 3 thread on paraphrase eval forced me to isolate exactly why paraphrase eval is higher signal than out-of-distribution eval, in a way the blog post alone did not require. The constraint was useful.
